# THE PULSE: Style & Editorial Guidelines
*Master guide for consistent, high-quality content creation*

---

## FORMAT & STRUCTURE

### Newsletter Summary (30-50 seconds to read)
**Purpose:** Quick-scan teaser that drives clicks to deep dive

**Requirements:**
- 150-200 words maximum
- 3-4 short, punchy sentences (Axios AM/PM style)
- One yellow highlight callout (key insight or surprise)
- Clear link text to deep dive article
- Ends with field marketing angle, not just trend observation
- Easy to scan — use short lines, active voice, minimize jargon

**Example tone:** "Shoppers are changing their minds at the register. Here's what field teams can do about it."

**NOT:** "Consumer behavior research shows that point-of-sale environments influence purchasing decisions in interesting ways."

---

### Deep Dive Article (3-5 minutes to read)
**Purpose:** Strategic insight + tactical implementation options

**Requirements:**
- 800-1200 words
- Lead paragraph answers: What? Why now? Why should field teams care?
- 2-3 main sections with subheadings
- Use data/research to support each claim
- Include specific examples tied to actual client products/channels (not generic retail)
- "What field teams can do" section with 2-3 concrete actions within their actual scope of influence
- Conversational tone that positions field teams as strategic, not just executors
- Clear takeaway that could be shared with leadership teams

**Structure example:**
1. Opening: The data/trend + why it matters now
2. Why this is happening (market context)
3. What it means for field marketing specifically
4. Concrete actions field teams can take (within their scope)
5. Closing: Competitive advantage if they move quickly

## FIELD MARKETING SCOPE OF INFLUENCE

### ✅ What field teams CAN do:
- Train sales associates on products and positioning
- Create in-store events and demonstrations
- Facilitate product comparisons side-by-side
- Move inventory through strategic placement/highlighting
- Create and maintain display units
- Gather feedback from store-level sales data
- Test new approaches at select locations
- Drive urgency through limited-time educational campaigns

### ❌ What field teams CANNOT do:
- Change pricing
- Create product bundles
- Make strategic business decisions
- Alter brand positioning
- Change which products are sold where
- Modify promotional terms set by marketing

**Content principle:** Always frame recommendations within their actual power. Avoid "you should convince leadership to..." unless there's a specific business case.

## EDITORIAL STANDARDS

### Data Recency
- **Rule:** Only use data/research less than 3 months old
- **Exception:** Seasonal patterns or foundational research if clearly labeled as historical context
- **Fact-check:** Verify publication dates on all sources; question any claim about "recent" trends without timestamps

### Novelty & Actionability
- **Must pass:** Is this something field teams didn't already know? Does it change their tactical approach?
- **Red flag:** "Here's a trend that's been happening for a while" → Reject unless there's a specific 30-60 day action window
- **Opportunity framing:** Lead with "competitive advantage if you move now" not "everyone else is doing this"

### Accuracy & Verification
- **Challenge assumptions:** Question unsupported claims about manufacturing locations, timing, economic projections
- **Source quality:** Prefer primary data (company earnings, Federal Reserve reports, academic research) over aggregator summaries
- **Flag uncertainty:** If a claim depends on thin sources, flag it for review before publication
- **Client relevance:** Examples must tie to actual products your subscribers sell (not generic hypotheticals)

### Competitive Advantage Framing
- **Good:** "Early field teams who implement this will outcompete teams who wait"
- **Better:** "By [specific action], your team can [specific competitive advantage] before [clear timeline]"
- **Avoid:** "This is interesting because..." or "It's important to understand..."

---

## TONE & VOICE

- **Confident and specific**, not speculative ("shoppers are" not "shoppers might be")
- **Direct and brief**, removing filler and hedging language
- **Strategic, not instructional** — position field teams as smart operators with leverage, not training audiences
- **Data-driven and urgent** — use real deadlines and competitive pressure, not manufactured hype
- **Show, don't tell** — describe what field teams can *do* more than what they should *think*

## DESIGN ELEMENTS FOR EMAILS/LANDING PAGES

### Summary (in newsletter)
- Subject line or headline: conversational, benefit-forward
- 150-200 word body
- One yellow highlight block with key insight
- Clear "Read the full analysis" or "See the strategy" link
- Can include small data visualization if it adds clarity

### Deep Dive Article (on landing page)
- Headline + subheading
- Body text with clear section breaks
- Inline data callouts or charts where they reinforce arguments
- "What field teams can do" as a distinct, scannable section
- Author: "The Pulse" or include your name if preferred

---

## RECURRING EDITORIAL CHALLENGES TO AVOID

**Overgeneralization:** "Retail is changing" → "Field teams at appliance retailers report that demo unit quality now drives 40% of purchase decisions"

**Missing the field marketing angle:** Describing a consumer trend without connecting to what field teams can influence

**Generic advice:** "Train your associates better" → "Use [specific product] side-by-side comparison to show associates how to answer 'why not the cheaper model'"

**Unsourced urgency:** "This is happening now" → "Tax credits expire March 15, creating a 6-week window for field teams to..."

**Repeating yourself:** If a topic echoes a prior article, either skip it or explicitly show what's new/different

---

## PUBLICATION CHECKLIST

Before sending to clients:
- [ ] All data sources are less than 3 months old
- [ ] At least 2 field marketing actions are clearly described
- [ ] Examples reference actual client products/channels
- [ ] Summary is 150-200 words and scans in 30-50 seconds
- [ ] Deep dive is 800-1200 words and reads in 3-5 minutes
- [ ] No unsourced claims or weak source flags have been addressed
- [ ] Tone is confident, not hedged or speculative
- [ ] Competitive advantage framing is clear and specific
- [ ] Deep dive includes "What field teams can do" section
- [ ] Link and layout are ready for email + landing page

## EXAMPLES TO EMULATE

These articles have worked well:
- Holiday 2025
- Part 179D Nest Thermostat Opportunity
- SELF-GIFTING SEASON: Why Durability Trumps Discount in 2025 Holiday Shopping
- THE SECONDHAND SHIFT: How Field Teams Turn Old Products Into Upgrade Sales
- Broken Displays Cost More Than Discounts Save

These elements made them work:
- **Specific deadline with verified external source** — 179D June 30 deadline, December 15 tariff deadline, July 24 Section 122 expiry; urgency is always externally validated, never manufactured
- **Recipient-of-the-action framing** — Articles show the field team why *they* are the competitive variable, not just describing a market trend
- **Before/after contrast with real scripts** — Consultative Selling and Warranty Positioning both show exact wrong language vs. right language side-by-side; readers can train on the spot
- **The math is always shown** — Every strong article includes a revenue impact calculation (e.g., 500 visitors/week × close rate lift × $400 = $X annually); makes the case to share with leadership
- **Narrow product specificity** — Holiday 2025 and 179D name actual SKUs (Nest thermostat, Pixel) rather than category generalizations; builds immediate relevance for field teams who sell those exact products
- **Emotional/behavioral insight first, tactics second** — Self-Gifting Season and Secondhand Shift both open with a consumer psychology finding that reframes the whole category, then transition to tactics
